## 4. Ordering Policies

### 4.1 FCFS

Sorts all transactions in the current 60-second window by `arrival_ms` (earliest first). Per-sender nonce ordering is enforced. The batcher then greedily packs in that order.

### 4.2 DES (DA Efficiency Score)

DES uses a fit-aware HOL (Head-of-Line) selection loop. For every transaction slot in the output:

1. **Filter:** keep only candidates whose `payload_size + current_batch_size <= 128KB`
2. **Score:** among fitting candidates, compute DES score and pick the highest
3. **If nothing fits:** close the current blob (`current_batch_size = 0`), open a new one, score from scratch

```
DES(tx) = α × wait_score + β × compress_score + γ × fit_score
```

**wait_score** = `(sim_clock_ms - tx.arrival_ms) / batch_timeout_ms`, clamped to [0, 1]
— Fixed per tx per window. Does NOT increment within a window — `sim_clock_ms` is the window end time and stays constant for the entire ordering loop. Wait_score only grows between windows, not within one.

**compress_score** = `1.0 - (payload_size / 10_000)`, clamped to [0.1, 1.0]
— Fixed per tx per window. Smaller txs score higher because smaller calldata tends to have more ABI zero-padding and compresses better.

**fit_score** = `payload_size / remaining_blob_space` if tx fits, else `0.0`
— Dynamic per iteration. Recalculates every time a tx is placed because `remaining_blob_space` shrinks. Non-fitting txs always receive 0.0 regardless of weights.

All three scores are recomputed on every iteration of the selection loop, but only `fit_score` actually changes between iterations. The loop repeats filter → score → place for every single tx until the mempool is empty.

### 4.3 Critical insight: the HOL filter drives blob savings, not the scores

The filter in step 1 is a hard constraint enforced before scoring. A transaction that does not fit in the current blob cannot be selected regardless of how high its score is.

This means:
- α/β/γ weights rank candidates **within the fitting set only**
- The weights have zero influence over whether a blob boundary is crossed
- All DES weight configurations produce the same blob count (confirmed by sweep)
- The weights are purely latency controls

If the filter were removed, blob count would become weight-dependent: high γ would softly replicate the filter via fit_score=0.0 for non-fitting txs, but high α or β could override it, allowing non-fitting txs to be selected and wasting blob tail space.

## 5. Results

All six runs completed end-to-end with real EIP-4844 blobs confirmed on-chain on the Reth devnet (May 7, 2026).

### 5.1 Primary comparison: FCFS vs DES across workloads

| Dataset | Policy | Blobs | Uncomp Fill | Ordering Avg | Ordering P95 | Ordering Max | Inclusion Avg |
|---------|--------|-------|-------------|--------------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| small_heavy | FCFS | 11 | 28.18% | 1,224ms | 7,471ms | 13,736ms | 5,787ms |
| small_heavy | DES | 11 | 28.18% | 1,232ms | 7,527ms | 13,736ms | 5,976ms |
| large_heavy | FCFS | 195 | 87.17% | 655ms | 2,748ms | 3,951ms | 5,978ms |
| large_heavy | DES | **178** | **95.50%** | 1,904ms | 9,143ms | 26,435ms | 5,998ms |
| mixed | FCFS | 20 | 79.15% | 1,349ms | 5,813ms | 19,116ms | 5,622ms |
| mixed | DES | 20 | 79.15% | 1,876ms | 8,028ms | 31,729ms | 5,577ms |

**Ordering latency** = estimated time from tx arrival to when the ordering algorithm selected it within the window, measured by spreading selection times evenly across the batch window weighted by output position.

**Inclusion latency** = time from blob tx submission to on-chain confirmation. Consistently ~5.7–6.0 seconds across all runs — one L1 slot = one blob included, completely independent of ordering policy.

### 5.2 Key findings

**Blob savings only occur on large_heavy (−8.7%).**

DES requires two conditions simultaneously:
1. **Blob overflow** — the window's total tx volume must exceed 128 KB. Without overflow there are no blob boundary decisions to optimize.
2. **Size variance** — tx sizes must vary enough that some txs fit in the current blob tail and others don't. Without variance the HOL filter has no discriminating power.

`small_heavy` fails condition 1: 500 txs × ~68B avg = ~34 KB/window, fits in one blob.
`mixed` partially fails condition 2: the large number of small txs means blob tails can almost always be filled by arrival order alone.
`large_heavy` satisfies both: 500 txs × ~2,468B avg = ~9.6 blobs/window, with 2,000–5,000B size range creating real fit decisions at each blob boundary.

**Fill rate improves alongside blob savings.**
large_heavy fill rate: 87.17% (FCFS) → 95.50% (DES), +8.33 percentage points. Tighter packing at blob boundaries means fewer partially-filled blobs.

**DES costs latency on large_heavy.**
- Avg ordering latency: 655ms → 1,904ms (3× increase)
- P95 ordering latency: 2,748ms → 9,143ms (3.3× increase)
- Max ordering latency: 3,951ms → 26,435ms (6.7× increase)

This is the HOL-blocking cost. Large transactions that don't fit in the current blob tail are deferred until a new blob opens. Each deferral adds wait time. Tail latency is the honest measure — average latency can be misleading because DES packs more txs into early blob positions, pulling the average down slightly even when tail is much worse.

**Inclusion latency is completely independent of ordering.**
~6 seconds for every run across all 6 experiments. This cannot be improved by the sequencer — it is determined by L1 slot timing.

### 5.3 Parameter sweep: why α/β/γ weights don't affect blob count

All DES weight configurations on large_heavy produced exactly 178 blobs:

| Configuration | α | β | γ | Blobs | P95 Latency | Max Latency |
|---------------|---|---|---|-------|-------------|-------------|
| FCFS baseline | — | — | — | 195 | ~59,000ms | ~62,000ms |
| pure_wait | 1.00 | 0.00 | 0.00 | 178 | ~58,000ms | ~60,000ms |
| pure_compress | 0.00 | 1.00 | 0.00 | 178 | ~80,000ms | ~99,000ms |
| pure_fit | 0.00 | 0.00 | 1.00 | 178 | ~84,000ms | ~101,000ms |
| equal (default) | 0.33 | 0.33 | 0.34 | 178 | ~68,000ms | ~73,000ms |
| wait_heavy | 0.50 | 0.25 | 0.25 | 178 | ~64,000ms | ~67,000ms |
| compress_heavy | 0.25 | 0.50 | 0.25 | 178 | ~69,000ms | ~74,000ms |
| fit_heavy | 0.25 | 0.25 | 0.50 | 178 | ~70,000ms | ~76,000ms |
| no_wait | 0.00 | 0.50 | 0.50 | 178 | ~83,000ms | ~101,000ms |

The weights only affect latency:
- High α → starvation prevented → lower tail latency
- Low/zero α → large txs deferred indefinitely → max latency exceeds the 60s window
- β and γ have secondary effects; neither saves additional blobs

**Best weight configuration:** `wait_heavy` (α=0.5, β=0.25, γ=0.25) — full 8.7% blob reduction with the lowest tail latency among all DES configurations.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Why DES saves blobs only on large-transaction workloads

The HOL filter has discriminating power only when some txs fit in the current blob tail and others do not. This requires:
- Tail space that is meaningful relative to tx size (overflow must exist)
- Enough size variance that the filter actually excludes some candidates

On `small_heavy`, every tx fits everywhere — the filter never excludes anything, so DES and FCFS produce identical output. On `mixed`, small txs fill blob tails naturally by arrival order, giving the filter little opportunity to activate. On `large_heavy`, the 2,000–5,000B size range means at each blob boundary there is a meaningful split between txs that fit and txs that don't.

### 6.2 Why compression doesn't save blobs on large_heavy

zstd compression is applied after packing. The ordering engine makes all decisions against uncompressed sizes. Large DEX calldata compresses at only 1.09:1, so compression saves ~11KB per 128KB blob — not enough to meaningfully shift blob boundary decisions. The β (compress_score) weight therefore has limited practical impact on this workload: compressibility variation within a window of similar-sized large txs is too small to differentiate candidates.

### 6.3 Why wait_score does not increment within a window

`sim_clock_ms` is passed into `order()` once and stays fixed for the entire selection loop. `wait_score = (sim_clock_ms - tx.arrival_ms) / 60000` is therefore frozen per tx per window. A tx that has been skipped 50 times within the same loop has the same wait_score as on the first iteration. Wait_score only grows between windows — if a tx is not selected in window N, it enters window N+1 with a larger `(sim_clock_ms - arrival_ms)` gap.

### 6.4 The latency cost is structural, not tunable

The HOL-blocking latency cost is inherent to the filter design. As long as the filter defers non-fitting txs, those txs accumulate wait time. Increasing α (wait_score) bounds but does not eliminate this cost — it ensures deferred txs eventually win, but they still incur the deferral wait. The only ways to fundamentally reduce this cost require architectural changes (see Section 7).

## 7. Future Experiments and Open Questions

The following experiments are ranked by expected insight and implementation effort. All are feasible within the current codebase.

### 7.1 Remove the HOL filter — pure score-based ordering (Very Low Effort)

**What:** Remove the single filter line in `ordering.rs:98`:
```rust
.filter(|(_, q)| q[0].payload_size + current_batch_size <= self.max_blob_size)
```

**Why:** This directly tests whether scoring alone (without the hard constraint) can achieve blob savings. With high γ, non-fitting txs receive `fit_score = 0.0`, which softly replicates the filter. But high α or β can override this, allowing non-fitting txs to be selected.

**Expected results:**
- pure_fit (γ=1.0): blob count likely stays near 178 — fit_score=0 effectively blocks non-fitting txs
- pure_wait (α=1.0): blob count likely rises toward 195 — wait_score overrides fit preference
- wait_heavy (α=0.5): somewhere in between — partial blob savings with lower latency cost than filter version

**What this answers:** Is the hard filter necessary, or can γ tuning achieve the same blob savings with better latency? If pure_fit without filter ≈ 178 blobs, the filter is redundant for high-γ configs. If it's significantly worse, the filter is essential.

---

### 7.2 Force-inclusion timeout — cap tail latency (Low Effort)

**What:** Track how many times each tx has been skipped in the current window. After N skips, force a new blob to open regardless of fit.

**Implementation:** Add a `skip_count: HashMap<u64, usize>` to the DES ordering loop. When a tx's skip count exceeds threshold N, treat it as a new-blob trigger.

**Why:** DES's max latency on large_heavy is 26,435ms — 6.7× worse than FCFS. This is the single biggest practical objection to deploying DES. A timeout directly caps this without changing the blob-saving mechanism for most txs.

**Expected tradeoff:**
- Low N (e.g. 3 skips): latency nearly identical to FCFS, blob savings reduced
- High N (e.g. 20 skips): blob savings preserved, latency capped at ~N × avg_skip_cost
- There is likely a sweet spot where blob savings are maintained at ~170–175 blobs with max latency under 10,000ms

**What this answers:** Can DES be made practically deployable by bounding its worst-case latency? What is the blob-savings vs latency-cap tradeoff curve?

---

### 7.3 Compressed mixed dataset — realistic overflow (Very Low Effort)

**What:** Regenerate `mixed.csv` with a 2-minute time span instead of 5 minutes. This creates ~2,500 txs/window instead of ~1,000, producing heavy overflow on a mixed-size workload.

**Why:** The current `mixed` result (no DES savings) is partly because 3.2 blobs/window with mixed sizes is easy for FCFS to handle — small txs naturally fill blob tails. At 2-minute span, the overflow is severe enough that fit decisions at blob boundaries become critical even for mixed traffic.

**Expected results:** DES may show 5–15% blob savings on the compressed mixed dataset. This would be the most practically relevant result — real L2 traffic is mixed-size, and the question of whether DES helps under real load is currently unanswered.

**What this answers:** Does DES help on realistic mixed traffic when the network is congested? The current experiment only tests moderate load.

---

### 7.4 Hybrid policy — FCFS with DES at blob boundaries (Medium Effort)

**What:** Use FCFS ordering normally, but switch to DES fit-aware selection only when remaining blob space drops below a threshold (e.g. < 20KB remaining). Above the threshold, admit txs in arrival order. Below the threshold, filter and score.

**Why:** Most of the blob savings come from the last few selections before a blob boundary. FCFS wastes space specifically because it selects txs that are too large for the remaining tail. A hybrid policy captures this saving while imposing near-zero latency cost on the majority of txs.

**Expected results:**
- Blob count: likely 180–185 (most but not all of DES's savings)
- Latency: nearly identical to FCFS for most txs, with small DES-like costs only at boundaries
- This may be the Pareto-optimal policy: better blobs than FCFS, better latency than DES

**What this answers:** Is the full DES loop necessary, or does fit-aware selection only at boundaries capture most of the value?

---

### 7.5 Pack to compressed size — batcher improvement (Medium Effort)

**What:** Change the batcher to pack toward the 128KB compressed size target rather than the uncompressed size. After each tx is appended, compress the current batch and check whether the compressed size exceeds the limit.

**Why:** Currently the batcher packs to 128KB uncompressed, then compresses. Small txs compress at 1.55:1, meaning a "full" 128KB uncompressed blob only uses ~82KB of actual blob capacity. Packing to compressed size would fit ~55% more small txs per blob, potentially reducing blob count on small_heavy from 11 to ~7.

**Trade-off:** Compressing after every tx insertion is expensive. A practical implementation would compress every K txs and check, rather than after every insertion.

**What this answers:** How much blob capacity is being wasted due to the uncompressed packing strategy? Is compression a significant DA lever for small-tx workloads?

## 8. Architecture Decisions

### Why the HOL filter is separate from scoring
The filter makes blob savings weight-independent. Every α/β/γ configuration produces 178 blobs on large_heavy. This is a deliberate design choice: blob count becomes a deterministic property of the algorithm structure, not a function of parameter tuning. The cost is that the filter is a blunt instrument — it defers txs that don't fit regardless of how long they've been waiting.

### Why compression doesn't save blobs on large_heavy
zstd is applied post-packing. The ordering engine operates on uncompressed sizes throughout. Large calldata compresses at 1.09:1, which is nearly incompressible. Even if the batcher packed to compressed size, large-tx workloads would see minimal improvement from compression because the ratio is too close to 1:1.

### Why average ordering latency is misleading
DES packs more txs into fewer, denser blobs. Txs in early blob positions have low individual latencies, pulling the average down. The max and P95 latency are the honest measures: they reveal the tail cost paid by txs that were repeatedly deferred because they didn't fit.

### Why wait_score is fixed within a window
`sim_clock_ms` is passed once into `order()` and does not advance during the selection loop. wait_score = (sim_clock_ms − arrival_ms) / 60000 is computed fresh each iteration but produces the same value because neither input changes. The only way a tx's wait_score increases is across window boundaries, when the next window's `sim_clock_ms` is 60,000ms higher.

## 9. Conclusions

DES is a conditional improvement over FCFS:

| Condition | DES benefit |
|-----------|-------------|
| Small txs, no overflow | None — identical to FCFS |
| Mixed sizes, moderate overflow | None — small txs fill tails naturally |
| Large txs (>2KB), heavy overflow | −8.7% blob count, 3–6× latency cost |

The 8.7% blob reduction on large_heavy translates directly to 8.7% lower blob fee cost. The latency cost (6.7× higher max latency on large_heavy) is real and currently unbounded — DES as implemented is not suitable for latency-sensitive workloads without a force-inclusion timeout.

The most important open question is whether the HOL filter can be replaced or augmented to reduce the latency cost while preserving blob savings. The five experiments in Section 7 address this from different angles, starting with the simplest: remove the filter entirely and observe whether scoring alone achieves comparable savings with better latency.

DES is most relevant for rollups with specialized large-calldata workloads (ZK proof posting, complex contract interactions, DA-heavy applications). For general-purpose L2s with real mixed traffic at moderate load, FCFS and DES produce equivalent blob counts under the current implementation.
